01 -- Problem

Water contamination is widespread.
Detection tools are out of reach.

Toxic chemicals are found in drinking water worldwide -- from algal blooms in warming lakes, to herbicide runoff in farm country, to industrial chemicals that never break down. These contaminants cause liver damage, cancer, and hormone disruption, even at trace concentrations.

The problem is not just contamination. It is that most communities cannot afford to test for it. Current lab methods cost $30,000 to $200,000 in equipment alone and take days to return results. A fast, affordable field sensor simply does not exist yet.

02 -- Engineering Goal

Could a CD become a water sensor?

Commercial optical discs (CDs, DVDs, Blu-rays) are manufactured with precision nanoscale grooves -- the same structures used in diffraction gratings, which are expensive optical components used in laboratory instruments.

The goal of this project was to test whether those ubiquitous, cheap discs could replace custom-fabricated gratings in a surface plasmon resonance (SPR) sensor, with enough sensitivity to detect real water contaminants. Three disc formats were compared under identical conditions.

03 -- Methodology

From optical disc to plasmonic sensor.

Each disc's grooves act as a diffraction grating. A thin metal film turns those grooves into an SPR sensor. Here is the full process.

Step 01
Strip and clean the disc
BD-R, DVD-R, and CD-R discs are cut to 2.5 x 2.5 cm. The original metal and dye layers are dissolved using 70% nitric acid (HNO3), exposing the bare polymer grating underneath.
Step 02
Deposit a metal film
Silver (Ag) or copper (Cu) is deposited by magnetron sputtering at thicknesses of 40, 50, or 70 nm. The metal film coats the grating grooves, forming the plasmonic interface where sensing happens.
Step 03
Illuminate and measure
Broadband p-polarized light from a lamp is focused onto the sensor at incident angles from 30 to 70 degrees. A spectrometer records the reflectance ratio (Ip/Is) at each wavelength.
Step 04
Track the resonance dip
When light couples into surface plasmons, a sharp dip appears in the reflectance spectrum. Its wavelength shifts when refractive index at the surface changes -- that shift is the sensing signal.

Understanding the physics

Surface Plasmon Resonance

A surface plasmon is a collective wave of electrons that travels along a metal-dielectric interface (for example, the boundary between silver and water). When incoming light hits the metal at just the right angle and wavelength, its energy couples into this electron wave -- and a sharp dip appears in the reflected light. That dip is the SPR signal.

The key insight: the dip position depends on what is touching the metal surface. If a molecule (like a toxin) is present, the local environment changes slightly, and the dip shifts. Measure the shift, and you measure the concentration.

How the grating couples light

Normally, light in air cannot couple into surface plasmons because it carries too little in-plane momentum. A diffraction grating solves this by adding discrete "kicks" of momentum to the light. Each kick is a multiple of G = 2pi / Lambda, where Lambda is the grating period.

Optical disc grooves are precision diffraction gratings, manufactured to nanometer tolerances for millions of units per year. They provide exactly this momentum matching -- at zero extra cost. The period (Lambda) differs across CD, DVD, and Blu-ray, which is why each disc type supports SPR at different wavelengths.

Refractive index sensing

The SPR resonance position depends on the refractive index (n) of the medium touching the metal surface. Pure water has n = 1.333. When a dissolved analyte (like a toxin or salt) is present, n increases slightly.

That small increase shifts the resonance dip to a longer wavelength. The sensitivity S = delta-lambda / delta-n (units: nm per RIU, where RIU = refractive index unit) tells you how many nanometers the dip moves per unit change in n. Higher S means better detection of dilute analytes.

Why grating-coupled instead of prism-coupled?

The classic lab SPR instrument uses a prism (the Kretschmann configuration) to couple light into plasmons. This requires precise angular control, bulky mechanical parts, and careful optical alignment. The instrument costs $50,000 or more.

A diffraction grating achieves the same coupling in a flat, compact geometry. No prism needed. No precision goniometer. The optical disc provides a free, mass-produced grating, making the entire apparatus buildable for ~$200.

Finding 01
CD-R gave the highest sensitivity
CD-R (Lambda = 1582 nm) achieved 394 nm/RIU. Its large period supports higher diffraction orders, producing broader resonance features with larger wavelength shifts per unit RI change.
Finding 02
BD-R gave the sharpest resonance
BD-R (Lambda = 322 nm) produced the sharpest, most clearly identifiable dips and best agreement with RCWA simulation. Its shallow grooves allow continuous metal bridging across ridges.
Finding 03
Ag outperforms Cu
Silver produced sharper, more identifiable resonances than copper across all disc types. Copper's larger optical loss broadens and weakens the SPR dip. Thicker Cu films (70 nm) partially compensate.

06 -- Discussion

What the results mean.

Sensitivity vs. resonance sharpness
The results reveal a fundamental tradeoff. CD-R achieves the highest sensitivity (394 nm/RIU) because its large grating period supports higher diffraction orders and less selective momentum matching. This broadens resonance features but amplifies the wavelength shift per unit RI change. BD-R achieves sharper resonances but lower sensitivity (321 nm/RIU). For practical sensing, sharper resonances allow more precise determination of the resonance position, which can partially offset the sensitivity advantage of CD-R.
Why front-side illumination is preferred
Back-side illumination was tested as a potential approach for sensing in absorbing solvents. However, propagation through the 1 mm polycarbonate substrate introduces interference effects and weakens the electromagnetic field at the metal-dielectric interface. Only BD-R showed reasonable back-side performance, due to its small period and shallow grooves. Front-side illumination is recommended for all current disc-based GC-SPR applications.
Silver vs. copper
Silver was the more effective plasmonic material due to its lower intrinsic optical loss. Copper oxidizes rapidly in air, forming Cu2O and CuO surface layers that broaden resonances and shift coupling conditions. Protective coatings such as graphene or MoS2 have been reported to stabilize copper films and could extend the useful lifetime of Cu-coated sensors in future work.
How this compares to existing methods
The full apparatus cost of approximately $200 compares favorably against $50,000 to $200,000 for commercial SPR instruments and standard lab methods such as HPLC and ELISA. Disc substrates cost under $1 each, are mass-produced to consistent optical tolerances, and are available globally. This positions disc-based GC-SPR as a viable platform for community-level water monitoring in resource-limited settings where lab access is unavailable.
07 -- Future Work

Next steps toward field deployment.

The current platform demonstrates proof-of-concept sensitivity. Several extensions are needed to move toward a deployable sensor for real water samples.

01
Selective surface functionalization
Current measurements detect bulk refractive index changes. Adding antibodies, aptamers, or molecularly imprinted polymers to the sensor surface would enable selective detection of specific toxins (microcystin, atrazine, PFAS) at environmentally relevant concentrations.
02
Copper film stabilization
Copper is more abundant and lower cost than silver. Applying graphene or MoS2 protective overlayers could prevent oxidation and extend sensor lifetime, making Cu-coated discs a practical alternative to Ag for field use.
03
Smartphone-coupled readout
Replacing the benchtop spectrometer with a smartphone-based spectral sensor would reduce apparatus cost below $50 and enable fully portable, field-deployable sensing. Compact grating-coupled SPR with smartphone readout has been demonstrated in prior literature.
04
Prism-grating hybrid
Incorporating a prism on the substrate side of the disc could enhance back-side coupling efficiency, potentially enabling sensing of strongly absorbing or colored water samples where front-side illumination is distorted by the analyte.
05
Real water sample validation
All current sensitivity measurements used controlled ethylene glycol/water solutions as refractive index standards. Testing in real lake, groundwater, and tap water samples with known contaminant additions is needed to establish practical detection limits and selectivity.
06
Multiplexed toxin detection
SPR imaging with different functionalized zones on a single disc substrate could enable simultaneous detection of multiple toxins in one measurement, reducing time and material per sample in field settings.
