How It Works

From disc to detection
in four steps.

DiscSPR uses light to detect toxins — no chemicals, no lab, no waiting. Here's how a CD becomes a precision sensor.

Coat the disc
A thin layer of silver (50 nm) is deposited on a CD, DVD, or Blu-ray. The disc's nanoscale grooves act as a precision diffraction grating — already manufactured to exact tolerances.
Shine light on it
White light hits the silver-coated disc at an angle. The grating diffracts light, coupling specific wavelengths into surface plasmons — quantum ripples of electrons along the metal surface.
Toxins bind & shift
Water droplets sit on the sensor. When toxins (microcystin, atrazine, PFAS) bind to the surface, they change the local refractive index — shifting the resonance wavelength by measurable nanometers.
Read the spectrum
A low-cost spectrometer captures the reflected light. The resonance dip position tells you exactly which toxins are present and at what concentration — in under 60 seconds.
What is a surface plasmon?
A surface plasmon is a collective wave of electrons that travels along a metal surface. When light hits a metal grating at just the right angle and wavelength, it "couples" into this wave — and the reflected light drops sharply. That dip is your signal.
Why use an optical disc?
CDs, DVDs, and Blu-rays have precisely engineered nanoscale tracks (320–1600 nm pitch) that function as diffraction gratings. They're mass-produced to optical tolerances, cost cents each, and are available everywhere on Earth — making them perfect sensor substrates.
What does refractive index mean?
Refractive index (n) measures how much light slows down in a substance. Pure water has n = 1.333. When toxins dissolve in water, they increase n slightly. SPR detects this tiny change — a shift of just 0.001 in n produces a measurable wavelength shift.
The Problem

Billions lack access
to water toxin testing.

Cyanotoxins, PFAS, and heavy metals contaminate drinking water globally. Accurate detection remains locked behind expensive lab equipment most communities cannot access.

Microcystin-LR
Produced by cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) during warm-water blooms. A potent liver toxin — linked to liver failure in humans and mass fish kills. Increasingly common as climate change warms lakes and reservoirs.
WHO limit: 1 μg/L in drinking water
Atrazine
One of the world's most widely used herbicides. Runoff from agricultural fields contaminates groundwater and streams. Linked to endocrine disruption — affecting hormones even at low concentrations. Banned in the EU, still used in the US.
EPA limit: 3 μg/L in drinking water
PFAS / "Forever Chemicals"
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances persist indefinitely in the environment and human body. Found in firefighting foam, non-stick coatings, and food packaging. Associated with cancer, thyroid disease, and immune suppression at very low concentrations.
EPA limit: 4 ng/L (2024 rule)

Key Finding
BD-R most sensitive
Blu-ray discs (320 nm pitch) achieved 142 nm/RIU — more than 2× better than CD-R at 62 nm/RIU. Smaller grooves couple light more efficiently into plasmons.
