The Problem

Water toxins are invisible — and detection is expensive

"1 in 3 people globally lack safe drinking water. State-of-the-art toxin sensors cost thousands of dollars — out of reach for the communities who need them most."

Surface plasmon resonance sensors can detect trace toxins with extraordinary sensitivity. But traditional SPR platforms require nanofabrication labs and $50,000+ equipment. We asked: what if a $0.50 optical disc could do the same job?

Our Approach

Turning everyday discs into precision sensors

Optical discs have nanoscale grating tracks built right in — the same structure needed for plasmonic coupling. We coat them with silver thin films and detect refractive index shifts from water toxins.

💿

Nanoscale grating built-in

BD-R (320 nm), DVD-R (740 nm), and CD-R (1600 nm) track pitches act as diffraction gratings for SPR coupling — no fabrication needed.

⚗️

Silver thin-film coating

Ag and Cu films are sputter-deposited onto the grating to form the plasmonic sensing layer. Silver outperformed copper in all tests.

🔬

Two illumination modes

Front-side vs. back-side illumination compared. Front-side gave cleaner, more reproducible resonances across all substrates.

📊

RCWA simulation matched

Rigorous coupled-wave analysis confirmed our experimental spectra and guided metal thickness optimization.

Project Timeline

Research milestones

From concept to publication — the journey of DiscSPR.

Phase 1
Concept & substrate selection
Identified optical discs as low-cost SPR grating candidates. Selected BD-R, DVD-R, and CD-R for their distinct grating pitches spanning 320–1600 nm.
Phase 2
Disc preparation & delamination
Developed a repeatable protocol to cut, delaminate, and clean disc substrates without damaging the nanostructured grating surface.
Phase 3
Metal deposition optimization
Tested Ag and Cu films at varied thicknesses (30–100 nm) using magnetron sputtering. Optimized for resonance quality and reproducibility.
Phase 4
AFM characterization
Measured grating period, depth, and metal morphology using atomic force microscopy to confirm structure integrity post-deposition.
Phase 5
Optical testing & RCWA comparison
Collected reflectivity spectra across refractive indices and angles, validated against rigorous coupled-wave analysis simulations.
Publication
Published in Materials, 2026
Research accepted in Materials journal — CD-R achieving 394 nm/RIU, the highest sensitivity of all tested configurations.

Impact

Making clean water safety accessible to every community on Earth

01

Affordable water safety

By using $0.50 optical discs instead of $1,000+ nanofabricated chips, DiscSPR can make water toxin detection viable for schools, clinics, and communities in resource-limited settings.

Cost reduction >99%
02

Adaptable sensing platform

The disc substrate can be functionalized with target-specific antibodies to detect heavy metals, cyanotoxins, pesticides, and pharmaceutical contaminants in water supplies.

Multi-analyte capable
03

Future: portable device

Next steps include microfluidic integration, protective coatings for field use, and a handheld reader — moving DiscSPR from lab bench to real-world environmental monitoring.
